§ 01

Introduction

The question of whether to write "criteria" or "criterions," "formulae" or "formulas," "stimuli" or "stimuluses" may seem trivial at first glance. However, for language professionals — lexicographers, educators, editors, and policymakers — these choices reflect deeper forces shaping the trajectory of English.

Greek and Latin plural forms are undergoing measurable change, and understanding the direction and speed of that change is essential for anyone responsible for standardizing or teaching the language.

This report investigates how and why certain nouns of Greek and Latin origin are shifting toward regularized English plurals while others retain their classical forms. We argue that two primary forces drive this variation.

①
Semantic or grammatical drift Words that undergo drift — particularly when escaping dominant ownership by a single specialist register — tend to regularize across all registers. "Data" is the clearest example of this phenomenon.
②
Cross-register migration without meaning change Words that cross between academic and general public registers without undergoing meaning change tend to remain contested, with both forms competing simultaneously.

Words that remain firmly within specialist registers, such as "fungi," tend to retain their classical forms regardless of increased public exposure.

To investigate these patterns, our team selected a set of Greek and Latin origin nouns spanning general usage and discipline-specific contexts, examining their usage patterns across corpora and time periods to produce data-driven projections for 2040.

§ 04

Rate and Direction Comparison

Having established long-term usage patterns in the corpus section, this section compares the rate and direction of regularization across our selected word pairs and examines what factors explain the variation.

Our analysis suggests that the selected nouns fall into three broad categories based on their regularization trajectory:

Fast Regularizers

Words that have undergone significant semantic or grammatical drift alongside escape from dominant specialist register ownership. Regularization occurred not merely because these words became more widely used, but because their grammatical identity fundamentally changed.

data media formula

Contested Forms

Words that cross between academic and general registers without undergoing fundamental meaning change. These show genuine competition between classical and regularized forms, with neither decisively winning.

formula syllabus stimulus criterion

Classical Retainers

Words that remain firmly under specialist register ownership. These show little meaningful regularization pressure despite increased public exposure, because their primary context remains scientific communication.

fungi larvae nuclei algae

Contributing Factors

Several additional factors likely contribute to variation within these categories:

🔍
Etymological transparency How obviously Latin or Greek a word appears to an average speaker may influence how much resistance speakers feel toward regularizing it.
🗣
Phonological awkwardness The phonological awkwardness of the regularized form — such as "stimuluses" — may slow adoption even in general registers.
📈
Frequency threshold effects (Zipf's Law) Words above a certain usage frequency enter everyday speech and face stronger regularization pressure. These factors merit further investigation beyond the scope of this report.
🏛
Register ownership Notably, increased cultural visibility — such as the recent popular interest in mycology — has not meaningfully accelerated regularization of "fungi," suggesting that register ownership is a more powerful stabilizing force than frequency of public exposure alone.
§ 05

2040 Usage Projections

Based on our corpus analysis and theoretical framework, this section presents data-driven projections for which plural forms are likely to be treated as standard by 2040. These projections are informed by three external forces we identify as primary drivers of change:

Globalization is accelerating contact between regional varieties of English and producing increasing pressure toward a simplified, accessible common standard. This pressure disproportionately favors regularized plural forms, as classical forms carry no communicative advantage outside specialist contexts and present an additional learning burden for international users.
The effect of AI on plural form standardization is more complex and corpus-dependent. Large language models are trained on existing text corpora, meaning they currently reinforce whichever form is already dominant in their training data. In practice this means AI tools are likely to simultaneously accelerate regularization in general registers — where regularized forms already dominate training data — while preserving classical forms in specialist academic contexts where those forms remain prevalent.

The net effect of AI on standardization will therefore depend heavily on what registers future training corpora prioritize. We hypothesize that without deliberate intervention in corpus curation and AI training methodology, AI tools will amplify existing frequency gaps rather than resolve them. We further recommend that regulations regarding the labeling and tracking of AI generated text be established, as discussed in the Recommendations section.
Rising global literacy, combined with the proliferation of digital content creation platforms, means that non-native English speakers now constitute the majority of active English content producers worldwide. Research in second language acquisition suggests that non-native speakers are sensitive to existing dominant patterns in the language they are acquiring, tending to reinforce high-frequency forms rather than innovating.

In the context of plural morphology, this is significant — regularized plural forms already dominate general usage and digital communication registers. This creates a self-reinforcing frequency cycle: as regularized forms become more frequent through non-native speaker usage, they become even more dominant in the data that future speakers are exposed to. This makes any reversal toward classical plural forms in general registers increasingly unlikely.

(cf. Verhoef et al., 2015 — non-native speaker effects on morphological patterns)

Projected Outcomes by Category

Fast Regularizers

Regularization complete
Words including data, media, and formula are projected to complete their regularization trajectory across all registers by 2040. The classical singular forms datum and stratum are projected to become effectively obsolete outside of narrow specialist academic usage. Dictionary entries for these words are expected to list the regularized or semantically dominant form as primary, with the classical form marked as archaic or specialist.

Contested Forms

Register divergence
Words including syllabus, stimulus, criterion, and phenomenon are projected to remain genuinely contested through 2040, but with diverging trajectories across registers. In academic publishing, classical forms are projected to retain dominance. In general public usage and digital communication, regularized forms are projected to become the de facto standard. Dictionary entries are projected to list both forms as acceptable, with explicit register guidance.

Classical Retainers

No meaningful change
Words including fungi, larvae, nuclei, and algae are projected to retain their classical forms as dominant across all registers through 2040. Specialist register ownership remains strong, public exposure has not produced meaningful regularization pressure historically, and the phonological awkwardness of some regularized alternatives such as "nucleuses" provides additional resistance.
§ 06

Recommendations for Dictionaries and Education Policy

This section presents practical recommendations for two primary audiences: lexicographers responsible for dictionary entries and labeling, and curriculum designers responsible for language education policy. We also raise an unresolved methodological question regarding the role of AI generated text in future corpus linguistics practice.

For Lexicographers

Fast
For fast regularizers such as data and media, list the regularized or semantically drifted form as primary, with the classical form marked as specialist or archaic where appropriate. Continuing to present these words as straightforwardly irregular plurals misrepresents current usage reality.
Contested
For contested forms such as formula, syllabus, stimulus, and phenomenon, list both forms as acceptable with explicit register guidance. Presenting only one form as correct is both descriptively inaccurate and practically unhelpful for users.
Classical
For classical retainers such as fungi, larvae, and nuclei, maintain classical forms as primary, with regularized alternatives noted as informal or non-standard where they exist.

For Curriculum Designers

Fast
Teach the dominant contemporary form as standard and present the classical form as historical or specialist context only. Teaching "datum" as the standard singular of "data" in general English curricula is no longer warranted given current usage patterns.
Contested
Present both classical and regularized forms explicitly, with clear register guidance. Students should understand that "phenomena" is expected in academic writing while "phenomenons" may appear in general contexts — treating one form as simply correct does students a disservice.
Classical
Classical forms should be taught as standard, with regularized alternatives acknowledged as informal where they exist. Curricula should explicitly address register-dependent variation as digital communication becomes an increasingly dominant register.

Unresolved Question: AI Generated Text in Corpus Methodology

We raise the question of whether major corpus based dictionaries should begin disclosing the proportion of AI generated text present in their source corpora. As AI generated content enters public discourse at scale, corpus frequency data becomes increasingly difficult to interpret without knowing what proportion reflects natural human language use versus AI output patterns.


We recommend that lexicographic institutions develop clear standards for identifying and labeling AI generated text in reference corpora. Furthermore, we recommend maintaining separate AI generated and human generated corpora to allow researchers to track divergence between natural language change and AI driven pattern reinforcement over time.


Without such measures, future dictionary entries risk reflecting AI language patterns rather than genuine shifts in human usage — raising the deeper question: if AI generated text dominates future corpora, are we measuring natural language change, or are we measuring the linguistic habits of machines trained on past human language?

§ 07

Conclusion

This report has examined the pluralization of Greek and Latin origin nouns in English, analyzing long-term usage patterns, rates of regularization, and the factors driving variation across words, registers, and disciplines. Our analysis suggests that the trajectory of any given noun is not random but is shaped by identifiable forces — most significantly, whether the word has undergone semantic or grammatical drift, and whether it has escaped dominant ownership by a single specialist register.

We identified three broad categories of words: fast regularizers, whose classical forms are effectively receding across all registers; contested forms, where classical and regularized plurals coexist in register-dependent competition; and classical retainers, where specialist register ownership continues to preserve classical forms despite increased public exposure. These categories, while not rigid, provide a practical framework for predicting future trajectories.

"Looking toward 2040, external forces — globalization, the rise of AI as a writing tool, and the growing dominance of non-native English speakers as content creators — will collectively accelerate regularization in general and digital registers while classical forms retain their position in specialist academic publishing."

Crucially, the self-reinforcing nature of frequency cycles means that words which have already begun regularizing are unlikely to reverse course, and the frequency gap between classical and regularized forms is projected to widen significantly in general usage.

Our recommendations to lexicographers and curriculum designers reflect this complexity — resisting the temptation to declare one form correct across all contexts, and instead embracing register-sensitive guidance that accurately reflects how English is actually used. We additionally raise the unresolved question of how AI generated text should be handled in future corpus methodology, as this issue has direct implications for the reliability of frequency data on which all such analyses depend.

"Ultimately, the story of Greek and Latin plurals in English is a story about who owns a word. When a word belongs primarily to specialists, its classical form endures. When it escapes into the broader language — carried by technology, culture, or sheer frequency — regularization follows."

Understanding this mechanism is essential for anyone responsible for documenting, teaching, or standardizing the English language in the decades ahead.
